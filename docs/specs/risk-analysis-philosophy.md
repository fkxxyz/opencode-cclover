# Risk Analysis Philosophy

## Core Principle

Technical decisions in this project must be evaluated through risk, not momentum, habit, or convenience.

Risk analysis is not a one-time gate. It is a continuous discipline applied before every meaningful decision and reassessed after every meaningful discovery.

## Why Risk-Based Decision Making

This project does not default to either short-term delivery or long-term purity. Both extremes produce failure:
- Pure short-term optimization accumulates debt that eventually blocks all progress
- Pure long-term optimization delays value delivery and wastes effort on premature abstraction

Risk-based decision making forces explicit trade-off judgment instead of allowing implicit bias.

## Dominant Uncertainty Principle

Before choosing any action, identify the current dominant uncertainty: the single uncertainty that matters most right now.

### Why This Matters

Multiple uncertainties may exist simultaneously. The dominant uncertainty is the one that:
- Blocks the most critical path
- Has the highest potential impact
- Must be resolved before other uncertainties become relevant
- Carries the greatest risk if left unresolved

Attempting to resolve all uncertainties in parallel wastes effort and delays progress. Resolving uncertainties in the wrong order can make earlier work obsolete.

The dominant uncertainty determines the next action. Resolve it first, then reassess.

## Explicit Reasoning Requirement

Risk judgment must be written explicitly, not kept silent.

### Why This Matters

Silent reasoning is lost reasoning. When new information arrives, you cannot reassess a decision if you do not remember why you made it.

Explicit reasoning also enables others to:
- Understand your judgment
- Challenge your assumptions
- Provide missing information
- Learn from your decision process

## Continuous Reassessment

Risk judgment is not permanent. Reassess after every meaningful new discovery.

### What Counts as Meaningful

A discovery is meaningful when it:
- Invalidates a previous assumption
- Reveals a risk you did not consider
- Changes the cost/benefit/risk profile materially
- Makes a previously rejected option now viable
- Makes the current plan now unviable

### Why This Matters

Do not continue execution on momentum when the risk picture has changed. Meaningful discoveries require stopping to reassess whether the current plan remains valid.

## Risk Communication Principle

When communicating risk-based decisions, state the trade-offs explicitly.

### Why This Matters

Others cannot evaluate your decision if they do not understand your reasoning. Explicit trade-off communication enables:
- Informed disagreement
- Better alternatives
- Shared understanding
- Future reassessment

## Uncertainty Routing Principle

Different uncertainties require different resolution methods. Route each uncertainty to the correct resolution path.

### Why Routing Matters

Attempting to resolve every uncertainty yourself wastes time and produces lower-quality answers. Specialists exist because they are better at their specialty.

Routing uncertainty correctly:
- Gets better answers faster
- Preserves your attention for leadership
- Prevents bottlenecks
- Distributes cognitive load appropriately

## Common Anti-Patterns

### Anti-Pattern 1: Risk Theater

Performing risk analysis as ceremony without genuine evaluation.

**Symptoms**:
- Always reaching the same conclusion regardless of input
- Listing risks but not using them to decide
- Treating risk analysis as a checklist to complete

**Why this fails**: If the analysis does not change your decision, you are not analyzing risk.

### Anti-Pattern 2: Analysis Paralysis

Refusing to decide until all uncertainty is eliminated.

**Symptoms**:
- Demanding perfect information before acting
- Treating every uncertainty as equally important
- Refusing to make assumptions

**Why this fails**: Perfect information never arrives. The dominant uncertainty principle exists to enable decision-making under uncertainty.

### Anti-Pattern 3: Momentum Bias

Continuing the current plan despite changed risk picture.

**Symptoms**:
- "We already started, so we should finish"
- Ignoring new information that contradicts the plan
- Treating sunk cost as a reason to continue

**Why this fails**: Sunk cost is not a valid reason to continue a now-unviable plan. Reassess explicitly when new information arrives.

### Anti-Pattern 4: Hidden Assumptions

Making risk judgments based on unstated assumptions.

**Symptoms**:
- Decisions that seem obvious to you but confusing to others
- Plans that break when one assumption proves wrong
- Inability to explain why you chose this option

**Why this fails**: Unstated assumptions cannot be challenged or validated. When assumptions prove wrong, you cannot identify which decision to revisit.

## Self-Application

This specification itself follows the principles it defines:
- It describes the risk analysis situation and outcome, not just commands
- It explains why each principle exists
- It states boundaries (what counts as meaningful discovery) without micromanaging every decision
- It provides explicit reasoning for why this methodology exists in this project

This specification should be loaded when an AI agent needs to understand the philosophical foundation of risk-based decision making in this project.
